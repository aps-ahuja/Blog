Mental health has been a topic of increasing concern in recent years, as more and more people around the world struggle with anxiety, depression, and other mental health challenges. In India, the situation is no different, and in fact, the cultural beliefs and societal expectations around mental health can make it even more difficult for individuals to seek help.

Mental health challenges in India are complex and multifaceted, with many factors contributing to the issue. One major challenge is the lack of mental health resources and infrastructure in the country. According to a report by the World Health Organization, there are only 0.3 psychiatrists per 100,000 people in India, compared to a global average of 1.2 psychiatrists per 100,000 people.

Additionally, cultural beliefs and stigma surrounding mental health can prevent individuals from seeking help, as many people fear being ostracized or discriminated against if they reveal their struggles as we noed earlier. Mental health challenges are often viewed as a personal weakness or a sign of spiritual or moral failing, rather than a legitimate medical issue.

According to a study by the National Institute of Mental Health and Neurosciences (NIMHANS), nearly 14% of India's population, or about 180 million people, are in need of mental health interventions. To tackle these, the government has started to take some initiatives.The Indian government has taken several steps to address mental health challenges in the country. In 2018, the government launched the National Mental Health Policy, which aims to provide accessible, affordable, and quality mental health care to all citizens. The policy also includes provisions for mental health education and awareness-raising programs, as well as measures to improve mental health research and data collection.

Beyond government initiatives, there are also many resources and programs available to support mental well-being in India. Traditional practices like yoga and meditation are widely recognized as effective tools for managing stress and improving mental health, and there are many centers and teachers throughout the country offering these practices to individuals and communities. But still after all of these the mental health sector in India is under-resourced and often overlooked in public policy and discourse. Even when resources are available, the stigma surrounding mental health can prevent people from accessing them.
